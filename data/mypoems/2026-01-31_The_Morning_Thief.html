The Morning Thief

I went out walking with a wolf

who lived inside my skull—

he growled along the frozen street,

all fuzz and snarl and pull,

and every step was *stomp* and *crash*,

a drum behind my teeth,

as if the pavement had a heart

that thundered underneath.


Then came the nails, the void, the dark,

the sound that scrapes the bone.

I passed the same house twice, I think.

I liked being alone

with something sharper than the cold,

something that knew my name

and didn’t flinch. We walked and walked.

The sidewalk felt like flame.


But then—a shift. A softer thing.

A voice like silver thread

that stitched the morning back together,

*don’t stop*, the needle said,

and so I didn’t. Slowed my pace.

Let the rhythm settle in

like honey in a cracked blue bowl,

like light across the skin.


Two thousand steps. A little more.

The wolf curled up and slept.

I came back home with frozen hands

and something bright I’d kept—


not stolen, really. Borrowed, maybe.

The morning didn’t mind.

It let me take exactly what

I needed, note by note and rind

by rind, the whole sweet fruit of it,

the cold, the thump, the thread.


Now it’s noon. The walk is done.


The wolf stirs in my head.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
