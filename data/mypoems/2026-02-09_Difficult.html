Difficult

Brian sat me down the way a doctor sits you down,

not unkindly, but with that particular gravity

that means the diagnosis isn’t yours to choose.


*They find you difficult*, he said.

*And haughty.*


As if conviction were a fever.

As if the asking of a better question

burned, and they who turned from it

could feel the heat beneath their learned

composure start to crack and peel.


I understood the second word at once—

the way a room will wince when someone stands

and clears their throat and wrings their hands:

*I don’t know what I’m talking about, but—*

then talks, and talks, and everyone forgives

the flag they flew before the fort,

laughing to lower the standard

so the standard never has to rise.

No surprise. I would not laugh.

That was the heat. That was the hot

of *haughty*—not a body but a thought

that would not cool to room temperature,

would not demure, would not perform

the warm and mild and dim

agreement that the emperor looked trim.


And *difficult*—that stranger charge.

My project was my own. A solo climb.

Their job was not to scale but call the time,

and when I gripped the rock myself

they named it insubordination

of a face that answers to no one.


What I cannot square, what I cannot solve:

we built the thing they said I would dissolve.


Not me alone—but more than small, my part.

We dragged the couches in. We ran the wires

for the games no one expected students to require,

though students are still spines and hands

and hands need rest, and spines need room to bend

toward something with no grade attached, no end

except the end of Tuesday afternoon.


The student commons. Say it slow—

*commons*. A word that means

it belongs to everyone who chose to show.


We made a place where people said

the thing they didn’t know

without the joke, without the cringe, the winking preface.

Where not-knowing was the starting place,

not the apology.


And the cohort that came up through those rooms

was the strongest the program had bloomed in years.

Not because of me. Because of what a commons does

when someone won’t be delicate

about the building of it—hauls the table in

and says *sit down* and means

*you’re welcome here, your questions and your din,

your doubts, your unfinished paragraphs, begin.*


Brian is gone now.

I sit with what he handed me that afternoon—

not a wound, I think. A rune.

A map drawn by someone who had walked

the same hall, talked the same talk,

heard the same word lobbed like a stone

and wanted me to know: the territory

is real, the label is not,

and *difficult* is just the sound

an institution makes

when it meets a wall

it didn’t build.
