Let’s Get Rich

Let’s get rich. I mean it—flush

with the kind of wealth that blushes

when you catch it at the door

holding flowers, holding more

than its arms were built to carry.


Let’s get rich the way that berries

do: by darkening, by sweetening,

by leaning into ripening

until the branch gives and the mouth

opens and the word comes out

that no one planned to say. Like *stay.*

Like *have you eaten yet today?*


We’ll party with our friends. We’ll press

our faces into happiness

so hard it leaves a mark. We’ll dance

with hips that never learned to glance

at clocks. We’ll hug like trees

whose roots have tangled by degrees

so deep that neither knows

where one begins, the other goes.


We’ll kiss. On foreheads. Cheeks. On years

of thinking tenderness appears

only when you’ve earned it.


We will raise the young ones—watch

them learn that fire is hot by touch,

not lecture. Bind their wounds. Then let

them near the fire again. Don’t fret.

A scar is just a body’s way

of saying *I was here that day.*


We will do the birthdays: candles

melting into cake, the handles

of the gift bags ripping, paper

everywhere, the yearly caper

of pretending we’re surprised

that someone we love is alive.


And funerals. We’ll do those too.

We’ll stand in rain and waterproof

nothing. Let the grief get in.

Let it soak us to the skin

and say, this is the price of loving

well—this wrecked and beautiful thing.


Let’s get rich. Let’s make the art

that no one asked for. From the start

let’s build what has no business

case, no revenue, no fitness

for the market. Let a song

cost everything and earn us nothing

but the shaking in the lung.


We will resist. Not with our fists

—with kitchens. With the insistence

that a table set for twelve

when you can only feed yourself

is not naïve. It’s math.

It’s Methuselah doing the math:

nine hundred sixty-nine good years

and every one of them appears

to say *the point was never length,

the point was width.*


We’ll buy the bread and give the bread away.

We’ll cook for people whose last names

we never learn. We’ll heap the plates.

Community will thicken—not

like walls, like trees. So gradually

you miss it happening. The way

a forest doesn’t know the day

it became a forest. Just one morning:

shade enough for everyone.


We won’t worry anymore.

Not because the world has fewer

things to fear, but because the table’s

full, the door is open, and the terrible

beautiful math of abundance

says: the more you give, the more the branches

bend with fruit you didn’t plant.


Let’s get rich. Let’s get so rich

we forget we were ever poor.

Let’s get rich. Let’s set one more

place at the table.


There’s always room for one place more.
