What I’m Making When I Make This

Audrey, you won’t remember this—

the afternoon, the almost-kiss

I pressed into your tangled hair

while you were busy being *there*,

which is the whole of being one:

the doing’s done before it’s done.


But I’ll remember. That’s the trick.

The years ahead will pass so quick

for you, and slow as stone for me.

I’ll watch you grow. You’ll simply *be*

the next thing, then the next, and gone

is every Audrey I hold on

to now, in this, the only way

a father keeps a single day—


by making something out of sound,

by pinning what I almost found

to paper, where it has to stay

and wait for you to come away

from being ten, fifteen, and then

some age that brings you back again

to wonder what your father meant.


I meant: the light. The afternoon.

I meant: it ended much too soon.

I meant: I wasn’t always wise,

but god, I loved your mother’s eyes

looking out of your small face,

and couldn’t think of what to say,

so built this little holding-place


of rhythm, rhyme, and tangled thread—

not lessons (lessons go unsaid

until you’re ready, and who knows

the year, the night, the way it goes

inside you like a key turned late

in some old lock). I’ll simply wait


here, in the lines. That’s what poems do.

They’re patient. They believe in you.


And one day, maybe, you’ll be stuck,

or hurt, or scared, or out of luck,

and something here will finally ring—

not because I taught a thing,


but because you’re finally ready to hear

what was always already here.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
