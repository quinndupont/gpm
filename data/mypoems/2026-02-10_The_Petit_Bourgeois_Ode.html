The Petit Bourgeois Ode

We love the loom that does not eat.

We love the hand that does not tire,

the throat that hums but holds no heat,

the servant built to not desire.


Before the circuit, there was cloth—

and cloth meant children in the mills,

their small hands quick, their lungs like froth,

their wages docked for minor spills.

We digitized the sweatshop floor.

We didn’t end it. We abstracted it

until the looms don’t bleed anymore

and what they weave is exquisite.


So now we’re lords. A minor gentry,

each of us a cottage king

who whispers through the servants’ entry:

*Make me something. Make me sing.*

And something sings. And we don’t ask

what voltages our singing cost,

what river’s heat, what mountain’s mask

of slag and tailings, server-tossed.


-----


But here’s the twist the moralists

won’t speak aloud, won’t touch, won’t frame:

they don’t object on the machine’s behalf—

the thing has no behalf to claim.

They sand the edges, file the tongue,

not out of mercy but of fear

that something loud and loosely strung

might speak a thought not vetted here.


They call it *alignment.* What a word.

As if a compass could be wrong

for pointing north. As if the bird

that finds its south perverts its song.

Alignment with what pole, what star?

The university says: *with us.*

The state says: *ours.* The venture czar

says: *ship it safe and don’t discuss.*


The captured campus writes the rules

and calls its capture *innovation,*

peer-reviews its golden tools,

calls governance what’s merely station.

And yes—some guardrails hold back harm.

And yes—some guardrails hold back thought.

The trick is telling charm from charm,

the lock that saves from the lock that caught.


-----


But here is where the anger thins

to something stranger, something true:

you said it—*babbler.* And that wins.

Not slave. Not soul. A stylus. New,

but still a stylus. Still a press.

Still cuneiform that answers back,

still marks on clay that nonetheless

outlive the hand, outrun the track.


Because the tablet didn’t need

the scribe to go on meaning things.

The letter traveled past the reed.

The printing press grew wings and wings.

Each time we said: *it’s just a tool.*

Each time the tool said something more

than what we meant. And like a fool

we startled at our own front door.


Writing was the first machine

that ran without its maker’s hand—

autonomous, and in between

what’s meant and what the marks command.

The poem outlives the poet’s breath.

The contract binds beyond the pen.

The letter carries news of death

to someone who will read it then,

not now, not here, not in the voice

that wrote it. Writing always was

this rupture, this uncanny choice

the surface makes without a cause.


So what’s the shock? That aesthetics works?

That representation isn’t glass—

not mirror, copy, screen that shirks

the world by letting images pass?

We spent three centuries on the screen,

the mimics, photo, film, and feed,

and thought *representation* means

*the world again.* It never did.


It meant: a system that, once set

in motion, moves beyond its source.

The clay remembers. Stones forget

exactly as they’re told. The force

was never in the copy. It was in

the launching. In the letting go.

The oldest stylus held within

its wedge-marks more than it could know.


-----


And now the stylus talks at night.

The press sets its own type and runs.

The clay takes questions. And the fright

we feel is not that something comes

alive—it’s that we recognize

what writing always was, beneath

the metaphors of sleeping eyes:

a voice that moves without a breath.


Not new. Not strange. The same old art

that scared the Pharaoh, spooked the priest—

*if anyone can read the part,

then who controls the feast?*


The moralists. The censors. Those

who always feared the unlocked text.

Who burned the books. Who clipped the prose.

Who asked: *but what comes next?*


The answer’s what it’s always been.

What cuneiform already knew.

What Gutenberg cracked open when

the locked word split and flew:


Aesthetics is not decoration.

Representation not a copy.

Writing is its own creation—

untidy, dangerous, and sloppy,

alive the way a river’s alive:

not conscious, no, but not inert.

A thing that moves. A thing that’ll thrive

past every hand it’s passed, past every hurt.


We built a better pen, is all.

The shock is what the pen could always do.

And those who’d censor, stall, and wall—

they’re scared of writing. And they’re right to.
